## 1. RQ3 — two failed approaches, then a pivot (1.5 min)

*Plain meaning: RQ3 went through two approaches before landing on something
that works. First, classifying teams into five named types — that failed.
Second, letting the data find its own natural groupings — that also showed
five types aren't real. So I changed strategy entirely.*

> "RQ3 went through two iterations before landing on something that worked,
> so let me walk through that quickly."

> "First approach: I classified each team into one of five types —
> cohesive, collusive, free-rider, and so on — by measuring how similar
> each team's rating pattern was to five hand-crafted examples. It failed:
> 102 of 105 teams ended up labelled 'cohesive'. The hand-crafted examples
> were too extreme to be useful, so every real team just fell into the
> least-extreme bucket."

> "Second approach: I used a method called Archetypal Analysis to let the
> data find its own natural types, rather than deciding them in advance.
> The idea is: instead of finding the average team in each group, it finds
> the most extreme teams — the ones at the outer edges of the data that
> best define what each type looks like. Every other team is then a mix of
> those extremes."

> "Then I tested how many real extremes the data actually supports — two,
> three, all the way up to eight — checking whether the same archetypes
> reappear if I re-run on a slightly different sample of teams. Only two
> were stable. At five, the archetypes changed completely each time —
> no better than random."

**Goal of this item:** she understands the five-type idea is dropped, and
why — it's not laziness, the data genuinely doesn't support it.

---

## 2. RQ3 — what I replaced it with (1 min)

*Plain meaning: instead of five labels, I now give each team a single
"how unusual is this team" score.*

> "Instead of five labels, I now give each team one number: how unusual
> its peer-rating pattern is compared to a normal team. I call it an
> atypicality score."

> "The useful result: the teams flagged as unusual have about ten times
> more disagreement between my different scoring models than normal teams
> do. So 'unusual rating pattern' reliably predicts 'the models can't
> agree on this team' — which is a sensible flag for an instructor to
> review that team by hand."

> "One caution I want to state plainly: this shows unusual patterns cause
> model disagreement. It does **not** prove it catches free-riding —
> that would need data I don't have. I'll frame it as the weaker, honest
> claim."

**Goal of this item:** she signs off that this change of approach is
methodologically OK.

## 3. The data-quality problem (1.5 min)

> "Two reasons. One: if even one person on a team didn't fill in the

> "After removing those, I'm left with only 18 usable teams, and only 6

**Goal of this item:** she registers that thin data is a real, headline
