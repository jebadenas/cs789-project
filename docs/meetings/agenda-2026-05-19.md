**Total time: ~12 minutes of talking.** Pause after each item for her to react.

## 0. Quick framing (15 sec)

> "Since we last met I changed how I'm doing RQ3, I found a data-quality
> problem worth flagging, and I've built the attack simulation. I'd like
> your input on three decisions by the end. I'll go through each."

---

## 1. RQ3 — the old approach didn't work (1 min)

*Plain meaning: RQ3 was about sorting each team into one of five
"personality types" of group. That sorting didn't work.*

> "RQ3 was originally about labelling each team as one of five types —
> things like cohesive, collusive, free-rider. The problem: almost every
> team got dumped into the same one label. So the labelling wasn't telling
> us anything real."

> "I checked whether the data actually contains five distinct kinds of
> team. It doesn't. When I tested it, the data only reliably splits into
> two groups, not five: one big group of normal teams, and a small group
> of unusual ones."

**Goal of this item:** she understands the five-type idea is dropped, and
why — it's not laziness, the data genuinely doesn't support it.

---

## 2. RQ3 — what I replaced it with (1 min)

*Plain meaning: instead of five labels, I now give each team a single
"how unusual is this team" score.*

> "Instead of five labels, I now give each team one number: how unusual
> its peer-rating pattern is compared to a normal team. I call it an
> atypicality score."

> "The useful result: the teams flagged as unusual have about ten times
> more disagreement between my different scoring models than normal teams
> do. So 'unusual rating pattern' reliably predicts 'the models can't
> agree on this team' — which is a sensible flag for an instructor to
> review that team by hand."

> "One caution I want to state plainly: this shows unusual patterns cause
> model disagreement. It does **not** prove it catches free-riding —
> that would need data I don't have. I'll frame it as the weaker, honest
> claim."

**Goal of this item:** she signs off that this change of approach is
methodologically OK.

---

## 3. The data-quality problem (1.5 min)

*Plain meaning: most of the data we have can't actually be used.*

> "This is the thing I most want to flag. We have 105 team records. But
> 66 of them have no usable information in them."

> "Two reasons. One: if even one person on a team didn't fill in the
> form, that team's data has a hole in it. Two: some teams gave every
> single teammate the exact same score — that looks tidy but it carries
> no information, it's just everyone clicking the same box."

> "After removing those, I'm left with only 18 usable teams, and only 6
> of those are fully complete. The root cause is simple: we only have
> two course runs of data, 2023 and 2024."

**Goal of this item:** she registers that thin data is a real, headline
limitation — not a footnote — and it sets up the next question.

---

## 4. The data question — the most important ask (1 min)

> "So my question: is there any more peer-assessment data I can get?
> Other semesters, other courses, anything? If yes, it would directly
> strengthen everything — more teams, less of this attrition problem. If
> not, that's fine, but then I'll write thin data up as a built-in
> limitation of the study, and I need you to agree that's the right call."

**Goal of this item:** a concrete yes/no on more data, and agreement on
how to handle it if the answer is no.

---

## 5. Attack simulation — progress update (1 min)

*Plain meaning: the part of the project that stress-tests the scoring
models against cheating. I built it this week.*

> "Separate topic: I've now built the attack simulation. This is the
> part that tests how well each scoring model holds up when students try
> to game it — for example a whole team agreeing to give each other top
> marks."

> "It's working, and there's already a clear result: the simple
> averaging model gets fooled by collusion, but the more advanced models
> shrug it off because of how they normalise scores. That's exactly the
> kind of finding RQ1 is supposed to produce."

**Goal of this item:** she knows the flagship question (RQ1) is now
unblocked and producing results — reassurance the project is on track.

---

## 6. Two extra attacks — sign-off ask (1.5 min)

*Plain meaning: the proposal lists four cheating scenarios to test. I
want to add two more. I need permission because it's beyond the
proposal.*

> "The proposal specifies four cheating scenarios to simulate. I'd like
> to run six. Two are extras, and both are backed by research, but
> because they go beyond the proposal I want your sign-off."

> "Extra one: a student who simply doesn't submit the peer form at all.
> I found that the advanced models accidentally *reward* a non-submitter
> — they end up scoring higher than people who did participate. That's a
> real flaw worth showing."

> "Extra two: a student who deliberately marks down the strongest person
> on the team to make themselves look better by comparison. It's
> different from the exclusion scenario already in the proposal, and
> it's supported by existing research."

> "I also looked at a third possible extra and decided against it,
> because it turned out to be the same as something I'm already testing.
> I didn't want to pad it out."

> "So: are you OK with six instead of four? Or would you rather I stay
> strictly with the proposal's four?"

**Goal of this item:** an explicit yes/no on the scope extension.
