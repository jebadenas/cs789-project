### 3. Case study walk-throughs

#### 3a. Team 6 — Caffeine Overload (source code, Δ=6.69)

Score matrix (giver column → recipient row):

```
                 Li    He    Holmes-Albert  Chen  Huang
Li              14    15     0             16     0
He              14    20     0             17     0
Holmes-Albert    4     0    10              0    20
Chen            14    15     0             17     0
Huang            4     0    40              0    30
```

**What's happening:** Holmes-Albert and Huang form an isolated mutual-inflation pair. They give each other 20–40 points and **zero** to the other three. Li, He, Chen rate each other normally (14–17) and also give zero to Holmes-Albert and Huang. Two completely disconnected sub-groups within one team.

The group report session is almost identical (Huang gives 50 to Holmes-Albert, 0 to the rest; Holmes-Albert gives 0 to Li/He/Chen).

**Why models disagree so much (Δ=7.06):** The zeroes from Holmes-Albert and Huang toward the majority group create extreme weight imbalances in PeerRank/PeerHITS. Baseline averages over the zeroes. WebPA rescales differently. Each model handles the sub-group isolation differently → high divergence.

**Why it's still classified Cohesive:** mean_rater_std is high (9+) but the other features — moderate Gini, moderate-to-high reciprocity — still land closer to the Cohesive prototype than to Conflict in Mahalanobis space. The synthesised Conflict prototype does not represent this "sub-group polarization" pattern.

**Possible new label:** "Sub-group / Polarized" — two factions within a team that refuse to score each other. Different from Conflict (high asymmetry between individuals) — this is a group-level boycott.

#### 3b. Team 16 — showcase poster (Δ=3.78, the PeerHITS amplification case)

```
       Wei    Ma    Xie   Yan   Wang
Wei    12     0     12    0     12
Ma     12     0     12    0     12
Xie    12     0     12    0     12
Yan    12     0     12    0     12
Wang   12    60     12   60     12
```

Ma and Yan gave all 60 points to Wang and 0 to everyone else (0 to themselves too). This was the PeerHITS amplification case from the last meeting. The classifier labels it Cohesive because the Gini (0.38) and asymmetry (0.64) are below the threshold of the synthesised Dominant prototype.

This is a known extreme case — the models disagree dramatically here (PeerHITS gives Wang 43.9 vs baseline 36.0). The classifier should flag this.

#### 3c. Team 13 — 400 OK (source code, Dominant, Δ=0.70)

```
       Liu   Li    Yu    Peng  Yin
Liu      8    2    10    10     6
Li      11   25    10    14    13
Yu      11   16    10     8    13
Peng    10    2    10    10     6
Yin     10    5    10     8    12
```

Li self-scores 25 (double the normal ~12) and gives only 2 to Liu and Peng. Meanwhile teammates give Li 11–16. **Negative reciprocity (−0.41):** the people who rate Li highly receive low scores from Li in return. Li is a dominant contributor — correctly labelled.

#### 3d. Team 12 — Omnipotent (showcase poster, Dominant, Δ=2.76)

```
       Liu   Wang  Zu    Ye    Deng
Liu     10    10    0     0     9
Wang    10    10    0     0     9
Zu      15    10   30    30    15
Ye      15    20   30    30    14
Deng    10    10    0     0    13
```

Zu and Ye give each other 30, give 0 to Liu/Wang/Deng. Another sub-group clique (same pattern as Team 6). Liu, Wang and Deng give 0 back to Zu and Ye. Labelled Dominant rather than Free-rider because Zu/Ye have high in-degrees (they give each other a lot) rather than low.

### 4. Discussion questions for Anna

#### 4a. Is "sub-group polarization" a missing label? (most important)

Team 6, Team 12, and arguably Team 16 all show the same structural pattern: two students (sometimes a pair, sometimes one outlier) exchange high scores while giving zero to the rest of the team, who reciprocate with zero. This isn't captured by Conflict (which is about directional asymmetry between individuals) or Collusive (which is about inflating the whole group).

**Questions:**
- Does CATME or the peer-assessment literature name this pattern?
- Should we add a 6th label "Polarized / Sub-group"?
- Or is this better characterised as a variant of Collusive (a subset colluding)?

#### 4b. AA says k=2 is the right granularity for this dataset

| k | RSS | Bootstrap stability |
|---|---|---|
| **2** | 1789 | **0.917** |
| 3 | 1565 | 0.682 |
| 4 | 1375 | 0.591 |
| 5 | 1203 | 0.500 |
| 6–8 | ↓ | 0.48–0.60 |

The elbow selects k=5, but **k=2 stability (0.917) is far above all other k values**. This may mean the dataset robustly splits into only two behavioural modes: "typical" (the 102 cohesive-ish teams) and "anomalous" (Teams 6, 12, 16 and the dominant/free-rider cases). The finer 5-label taxonomy may be over-specified for 105 data points.

**Questions:**
- Report both k=2 (most stable) and k=5 (elbow) as findings, or commit to one?
- Is the binary split "typical / anomalous" more useful for Anna's practical use case (instructor flagging) than a 5-label taxonomy?

#### 4c. The classifier misses the worst cases — what to do?

The three most instructive teams (Team 6 group report Δ=7.06, Team 16 Δ=3.78, Team 12 Δ=2.76) are all labelled Cohesive. The classifier is not wrong in a technical sense — these teams are closer to the Cohesive prototype than to our synthesised Conflict or Dominant prototypes — but the labels don't match what an instructor would call them.

**Options:**
- (A) Add a Δ percentile threshold (e.g., >90th percentile = flagged regardless of label). Teams 6 and 16 fall above this regardless of their classification.
- (B) Calibrate synthesised archetypes using the real-data percentile boundaries rather than idealized extremes.
- (C) Use AA k=2 as the primary binary classifier (typical / anomalous) and apply the 5-label system only as a secondary characterisation for the anomalous cluster.

**What we lean toward:** (C) for the dissertation — the k=2 AA finding is the more interesting scientific result, and the 5-label taxonomy can be discussed as future work or applied to a larger dataset.

#### 4d. Carried from April: sequencing vs attack simulation (#9)

The team-dynamics labels are now available. The question remains: should attack simulation (#9) come before or after we finalise the classification? The argument for doing #9 now: we can test how IWF models behave when a synthetically manipulated matrix looks like Team 6 (sub-group polarization).
